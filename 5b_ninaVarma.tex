\lettrine{N}ina watches as her dog waddles across the room, his belly rounding out where once there had been a sleek, lean muscular little body. She used to joke about how alike they were: both restless, both pacing creatures. But she sees now all too clearly how they have diverged. She has to meet Eleanor later for an investor prep and is trying her best not to project anxiety onto her chubby little friend who is already too burdened. Nina, by contrast, gaunt—bones sharpening beneath her skin, collarbones Rhodesian ridge-back like is a picture of wrought restraint.

\smallskip

\textit{"You are what you eat, but it is just as true that you are what you don’t} 

\smallskip

Nina is thinking of Celiac and that allometric law her biologist friends speak of: the law of Kleiber, the mystical \( \tfrac{3}{4} \) law in which  basal metabolic rate (BMR) scales sublinearly with body mass:
\[
\text{BMR} \propto M^{3/4},
\]

This \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) power\footnote{Accordingly, according to the space-filling transport theory (West, Brown, and Enquist, 1997), such networks approximate 4-dimensional scaling behavior.} scaling arises in bodily models in which metabolic energy is delivered through hierarchical, fractal-like networks such as the circulatory system whose aim is to maximize delivery of blood or reception of oxygen and the likes.

\smallskip

Nina, weighing in at just 40\,kg, feeds her bloated French bulldog greens, lentils, tofu; as such becoming a proxy for her own restraint. She thinks of her dog as her co-pilgrim. She eats less; the dog eats more. It is as if he has taken on the appetite she has abandoned. An absurd Dorian Gray-esque duality: her weight shed, his weight gained, their metabolisms only together abiding to the tidy laws of \href{https://colab.research.google.com/drive/1xxmtmJSuziXUUPYG60LAZeY_pc6_tkZ9?usp=sharing}{Kleiber}:\footnote{Here is a further dataset of \href{https://animaldiversity.ummz.umich.edu/quaardvark/search/}{mammalia} for the reader to play}.

\smallskip

She doesn’t know when it started, exactly. Maybe when the anxiety had nested so deeply in her that hunger itself became an afterthought. Or perhaps, it was when she realized that feeding the dog was easier than feeding herself. His meals were an act of certainty, precise in their portions, regular in their timing. Hers were negotiations; something to be put off, something to be reconsidered. Something did not add up though.

\smallskip

What Nina does not know is that Srinivasa, her boyfriend, has been slipping the poor little mut choice cuts of beef all along. The dog has had his intake doubled: kale at dawn, brisket by dusk and being 12\,kg now, waddles in quiet contradiction, a beast cleaved from matter alone, a creature fattened by duplicity. He may well be her proxy, but he is also a chubby cipher of betrayal: a metabolic farce.

\smallskip

Nina had long suspected some late night shenanigans but was content that she and the dog, as a whole: 12\,kg and 40\,kg fit closer to a \( \tfrac{2}{3} \) law: \(
\text{BMR} \propto M^{2/3}.
\) Not a fractal world of alveoli, but the tidy surface-bound elegance of differential geometry.

 Heat loss. Contact. Shade. A truth measured not in arteries, but in skin, not in vitality, but exposure. 

 \smallskip
 Their position on the sub-linear power curve has flattened as he has fattened. Nina convinced as the puppeteer, smiles at the simple physics of their harmonious surface-to-volume dance and that Kleiber never turns in his grave. And all this amounts to is that Nina and her French bulldog sit somewhere between a \textit{wattled bat} and a \textit{sloth bear} on a log-log plot of BMR versus mass:

\smallskip

A similar scaling pattern appears in life history traits. Empirical data show that the maximum lifespan (\(L\)) of \href{https://colab.research.google.com/drive/1Y3mBHsijZOGWl_zzV1k4ORy6pm9dvW_d?usp=sharing}{mammals scales sublinearly} with the age at first reproduction (\(A\)) as \(
L \propto A^{0.7},
\)
implying that species that mature later also tend to live longer, but not proportionally. The exponent \(\approx 0.7\) emerges from both traits independently scaling with body mass:
\[
A \propto M^{0.15}, \quad L \propto M^{0.20}
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
L \propto A^{0.15/0.20} \approx A^{0.7}
\]
Nature trades off as it does: longer-lived species delaying reproduction, but not as much as their increased lifespan would allow such are the energetic and ecological constraints that dictate the delicate balanced dance between survival, reproduction, and growth. Accordingly, Nina will never have that first birth, at least not with Srinivasa.

\smallskip

Her French bully eats contentedly (for now) because he trusts that she will continue to provide.  She, however, does not eat because she is not content. Because of late, the act of refusing nourishment has become the control with which she has otherwise mostly forsaken. Every skipped meal an act of defiance against the sub-conscious self whose company she is no longer enjoying. The dog grows fatter, content enough with his submission to the rhythms of hunger and satisfaction. Nina gathers his lead, and he dutifully follows.

\subsection*{Nina Unleashed}

\lettrine{A} quiet café, in the heart of Nina's Brussels for  a pre-meet before their flight out to Mumbai is the setting. Eleanor had chosen the location with intent. Somewhere private enough to press for insight, to gauge how compromised Nina might be. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Illustrations/vign-dog.png}
    \caption{Nina's dog, \textbf{I}. Her embodiment of control, dependency, and hunger.}
    \label{fig:dog}
\end{figure}

News of the collapse of Chris's Invariant fund had made its way even to Eleanor's loftier environs. Some within her invited Circle may be caught up in it and she was hoping that Nina, being a bit more inclined that way may have some gossip. And yet, when Nina arrived, she did not come alone. She came with her dog, in all his chubbiness, small, alert, and tethered to a lead that she held lightly, almost absentmindedly.

Eleanor arching a brow. \textit{"I didn't know you kept a pet."}

Nina suppressing a full smirk but discernably amused, takes a seat opposite her, the dog curling up beneath the table at her feet. \textit{"I didn’t, until recently."} She reaches down, scratching it behind its ears. \textit{"His name is 'I'."}

\smallskip

Eleanor blinks. \textit{"I?"}  

\smallskip

Nina nods. \textit{"Yes, 'I'."} A pause. \textit{"Short for 'I am'."}

\smallskip

Eleanor, sensing that there was more to this than mere wordplay, studies her carefully. \textit{"And why that name?"}

\smallskip

Nina exhaling perceptibly enough, while absentmindedly winding the dog’s leash around her forearm. \textit{"Because he is utterly beholden to me. I decide when he eats, when he walks, when he sleeps. His entire world is dictated by my presence, and yet—"} she gestured downward, where the dog lay resting, \textit{" ... he adores me for it. Do you know what happens when I come home?"}

\smallskip

Eleanor shakes her head, perceptibly.

\smallskip

\textit{"He is overcome with happiness."} Absolute, tail-wagging, tongue-out, manic joy. \textit{"But it's not the kind of joy you or I would recognize. It's something else. A revelation. A religious experience. Because every time I walk through that door, it means he will eat. It means his world will resume."}  

Eleanor frowning, but in contemplating her search for purposeless purpose found herself rather piqued.

\smallskip

Nina smiling, but now with little warmth; perhaps not such a benevolent master. \textit{"I've realized that his happiness is built on his fear."} She let the words settle. \textit{"He is not free to control his fate. But in giving up control, he has been given something else: certainty. He doesn’t have to worry about where his next meal comes from—only whether or not I appear. And so when I do... I am his God."}

\smallskip

Eleanor lurching a little dramatically back into her chair as if to absorb this, performative as she has become of late. \textit{"And why 'I'? Why name him after the self?"}

\smallskip

Nina smirking again, the leash coiled now further around her fingers that were now discernably beginning to pale up. \textit{"Because I wanted to see if I could bring myself to say to him: 'I am hungry', 'I need to be walked', 'I am waiting for my master to return'."} She chuckled, darkly. \textit{"It turns out, it gets easier each time."} 

\smallskip

This is, at least the story she is telling herself.

\smallskip

Eleanor studies Nina precept-ably, for the first time unsure whether Nina was confessing something about the \textit{dog} or about herself.

\smallskip

The café remained still, save for the arrhythmic chiming of cups and saucers interminably being washed and shuffled as Nina's words chimed amongst them.  Eleanor sat back, arms crossed now, expression a measured unreadable. The dog—\textbf{I} ... shifting at Nina’s feet, was obviously sensing a change in convivilaity and tone.  

\smallskip

Eleanor, finally speaking: \textit{"You say his joy is a prisoner’s joy. That it’s the happiness of one who has surrendered control. But tell me ..."} she leans forward slightly, eyes now discernably pinched, \textit{"is that not exactly what we all crave?"}

\smallskip

Nina's turn to balk, intrigued but guarded in equal measure. \textit{"Crave? You think we want to be shackled?"}

\smallskip

Eleanor, unfazed: \textit{"We don’t want to chase. Not really. We don’t want to wake up every day wondering where the next meal will come from, which way the wind will shift, if the forces of randomness will be kind or cruel. We want that burden lifted."} She gestures towards the dog. \textit{"\textbf{I} is free from that terror, the horror that comes from uncertainty. And he loves you for it. He doesn’t fear his dependence, rather he revels in it. And deep down, Nina, so do we."}

\smallskip

Nina exhales through her nose, shaking her head. \textit{"You assume that safety is worth the cost. But what’s left when control is stripped away?"} She lifted the leash ever so slightly, catenary-like, all hyperbole, it hangs between them chain-like. \textit{"Is this his security, or his noose?"}

\smallskip

Eleanor, resolute: \textit{"The chase, Nina. The chase is the noose."}

\smallskip

Nina's turn now to narrow her eyes.  

\smallskip

Eleanor, pressing on: \textit{"Think about it. The horror of control, of agency, of the need to hunt "} she taps the table with each word, rhythmic, insistent, (the dog looks up expectant of a treat or a clip presumably) \textit{"it demands \textit{constant vigilance}. The knowledge that one mistake could mean ruin. That if we fail, no one will feed us but someone may catch us. That is the weight we carry. The weight of our ancestry: when we took the risk to come down from the branch onto the Savannah. Then as now we take the risk to rise from our pillowed, duveted security for the thrill of the chase."} 

She exhales, shaking her head. \textit{"And we are tired. All of us."}

\smallskip

Nina, her voices softer, huskier now: \textit{"So you’d rather be led?"}

\smallskip

Eleanor, for all her instincts, wasn’t sure. But something in the metaphor was distinctly unsettling her. The question she had brought Nina here to answer: whether or not the collapse of the fund had corrupted the Merriweather Circle, now seemed small in comparison. Because Eleanor was starting to suspect that the group, as everyone is, endemically compromised.
Not by finance. Not by betrayal. But by the deeper, more insidious reality that creeps among us all. \textit{The Hunger for Control.}
And somewhere along the way, we, they had each learned to love their leash. Eleanor, softly but without hesitation: \textit{"No. I'd rather someone else chased for me."}

\smallskip

And little more could be said after that. And a little less than that was said so they found that their meeting was over. 

\smallskip

The prize for this days endeavors was Nina's sharing of the false knowledge that Nina's dog was so beholden to her. That, in reality her perpetual re-arrival into the dog's life just marked the moment when he would receive his \textit{hors d'oeuvres} before the meat course proper (which would arrive later in the evening when she was napping). The dog was not so Stockholmian in his syndrome-driven adulation at Nina's arrival. He had diversified his funding by winning over Srinivasa with his cute head bobs and artfully restrained whining.  This transpired every day. What transpired just now was that Eleanor forgot the noise around the Invariant fund but now knew that at the Mumbai meeting Nina should take the \textit{lead}.

\section*{Tethering Secure Telecommunications}

\lettrine{T}{he} boardroom of \textit{Varma Telecom Venture}s is a pristine, high-ceilinged, homage to colonial pasts fitted out with the shiniest  mahogany table and a group of high-powered Indian investors. Just aside the helm, Eleanor resides with a blessed slide deck, no less, currently displaying a lattice structure derived from quadratic figurative residues. She is noting that the meeting comprisess all suit types. All execs, no engineers. "Let the dumbed-down, top-down deluge of derivative knowledge circulate", she mulls.

\smallskip

Suit 1: (Adjusting his cufflinks, tone skeptical) \textit{“So, let me get this straight. You’re ultimately telling us that RSA ... which has secured our industry for decades ... is doomed, and your solution involves... triangles?”}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{Illustrations/vign-berlinBoard.png}
\caption{All eyes on Nina with Eleanor tele-communciating in person}
\label{HelgotEuler:fig}
\end{figure}

Nina, never to be led but led to interjecting as planned, keen, sharp-eyed

\smallskip

\textit{ “Not just any old triangles. Figurative quadratic residues. They form the simplex, lattice structures that inherently resist factorization-based decryption. Classical cryptanalysis would require exponential time to break them. But unlike RSA, where Shor’s algorithm makes quantum attacks inevitable, our approach exploits natural modular structures that remain resistant even with qubit scaling.”}


Suit 2: \textit{(Glancing toward Eleanor, more in the hope that she might dial down the technicalities)} “You mentioned IBM’s advances in quantum computing. Realistically, how long do we have before RSA collapses?”

Eleanor: (Clicking the next slide, showing leaked projections of post-quantum decryption efforts) \textit{“IBM’s latest qubit stability has already reached a point where stored encrypted data, that is to say ... ‘data at rest’ ... will be decrypted retroactively. Governments and corporations alike have been \textit{hoarding} data, knowing they’ll be able to break today’s encryption in the future. We’re not talking about \textit{if} RSA collapses. It already effectively has.”}

\smallskip

Suit 3: (Shifting, as he becomes acutely aware of his taught trouser crotch, eyes narrowing) \textit{“Ahem, so what makes your system any different?”}

\smallskip

Nina: With a smirk, flipping to a lattice structure diagram \textit{“Quadratic residues define \textit{inherently non-trivial} packing within finite fields. What we’re proposing is using the symmetry within figurative numbers— hexagonal, triangular, and centered polygonal sequences— to form cryptographic lattices that can withstand even quantum decryption.”}

\smallskip

Suit 1: (Skeptical, arms crossed), \textit{“But where’s the \textit{assurance}? Ramanujan once speculated deeply on prime structures, but he did not develop post-quantum encryption. If I’m to pour millions into this, I pretty much need certainty."}

\smallskip

Nina: (Coolly) \textit{“Ramanujan is a name I’d always be happy to appeal to. We are working on the idea that primes form an unavoidable residue field at higher orders. We are operating in fields where even Ramanunjan would work up a sweat.”}

\smallskip

Eleanor: (Sharply, cutting her up in case the barbs were landing)\textit{ “Now is where we are at. And the question isn’t whether you believe in it — it’s whether you’re prepared for what happens when your data is no longer secure.”}

\smallskip

Suit 1: (Cold, final) \textit{“We’re not convinced. You’re asking us to replace an entire cryptographic foundation on an unproven conjecture. That’s a gamble, and we don’t gamble with our networks.”}

\smallskip

The conversation then  turns to infrastructure and matters piping, all of which the suits have been primed by requisite experts in prior meetings. They seemlessly and elegantly pontificate on matters packet prioritisation, and the likes. As they feel they have acquired sufficient jargon for the day, the shift moves away from questioning the requisite cryptographic experts in the room to how they will play forward these nuggets as their own in their next meeting.

\smallskip

\noindent A heavy silence then fills the room as the suits' derivative knowledge had finally effluenced away. Nina exhales, shuts her laptop. Eleanor ever culturally sensitive catches her cue. Without another word, they begin collecting their papers.

\smallskip

Nina: (Under her breath, bitterly bemused) \textit{“Wait until the breach happens they’ll be pedalling their half-baked misunderstandings and beg for an audience with us to refine the spin .”}

\smallskip

Eleanor: (Shouldering her bag, throwing one last glance at the investors) \textit{“And by then, it’ll be too late.”}

\section*{Under the Long Quantum Cubit Shadow}

Nina and Eleanor stepped out of the hushed glass doors of the consortium meeting, both still holding their lanyards as if they might be called back in at any moment. The meeting had skirted around our infrastructure's real vulnerability as ballet dancers avoid a dodgy floorboard. The room they had left was now humming with residual jargon: latency tables and something about carrier neutrality. There was the odd bit of misogyny amid the mahogany too. They walked slowly, their feisty little three quarter coat numbers barely brushing, caressing each other so slightly as they marched. 

\smallskip


\textit{"That got away from itself,"} Nina murmured finally.

\textit{"Completely untethered,"} Eleanor replied. 

\textit{``I don't know why I expected more,''} Nina said, finally.

\textit{``More than a checklist of legacy systems, half-hearted 6G promises and buzzwords?''} Eleanor raised an eyebrow. \textit{``The quantum threat barely got traction.''}

\smallskip

Nina nodded. \textit{"And yet it's the one thing none of them can prepare for."}

\textit{"Did you hear of the number being floated in the MIT seminar this week?"} Eleanor asked. \textit{"A hundred thousand qubits."}

\smallskip

\textit{"Yes I heard,"} Nina said. \textit{"That’s not simulation-grade anymore. That’s brute-force territory."}

% \textit{``Is anything secure then?''} Nina asked. \textit{``Once we have that kind of brute force capacity... Shor’s algorithm doesn’t leave much standing.''}

\textit{``Precisely,''} Eleanor said flatly. \textit{``RSA collapses. ECC too. Anything with a discrete log or factorisation base gets shredded. Grover’s search won’t help either if we pretend brute force is just a scaling problem. Even symmetric encryption isn’t immune, only dented less.''}

\smallskip

\textit{``And yet,''} Nina added, \textit{``we spend more time discussing spectrum auctions than post-quantum algorithms.''}

\smallskip

\textit{"If we hit that threshold,"} Eleanor continued, \textit{"then no lattice-based post-quantum algorithm is safe. Not from specialised search techniques, not from Shor’s descendants."}

Nina stopped walking. \textit{"There’s something deeply ironic about spending an hour arguing a toss over LTE fallback protocols while the world's cryptographic infrastructure is on a countdown clock."}

\smallskip

Eleanor offered a thin smile. \textit{"Like worrying about the wallpaper while the floorboards are on fire."}

\smallskip

\textit{"And none of them want to speak the real fear aloud,"} Nina said. \textit{"Not just the breaking of RSA or ECC, but the undoing of trust itself. The idea that identity and verification could become fluid."}

\textit{"Or retroactively unsecure. Everything we’ve encrypted until now---"} Eleanor trailed off.

\smallskip

They looked at each other.

\smallskip

\textit{``Because admitting the threat means rewriting the whole infrastructure,''} Eleanor replied. \textit{``No one wants to draw the curtains back and see how unprepared we really are. Not when so much money is sunk into existing models.''}

\smallskip

\textit{``We need more than lattice obfuscation proposals and NIST competitions,''} Nina murmured. \textit{``We need new models of security: resilience born of entropy, not just obfuscation due to obscurity.''}

\smallskip

\textit{``But entropy doesn’t pitch well to shareholders,''} Eleanor replied, finishing her tea. \textit{``And quantum readiness doesn’t come cheap or fast. Especially when the system doesn’t believe the future is already here.''}

\smallskip

\textit{"So what do we do?"} Nina asked, though her tone suggested she already knew the answer.

\textit{"Build for entropy,"} Eleanor said. \textit{"Not certainty. We have to start designing systems that accept collapse as part of the equation."}

\smallskip

Nina breathed out slowly. \textit{"And keep feeding the dog."} Eleanor raised an eyebrow.

\smallskip

\textit{"Metaphor,"} Nina clarified. \textit{"For the systems we love but no longer trust."}

\smallskip

Eleanor nodded. \textit{"Then let’s keep feeding it. But quietly prep the ark."}

Eleanor envisaged the decks of network servers in the bowels of buildings that they passed. The substrate for our constipated societal exchanges.
